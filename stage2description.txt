# Stage 2 — Mutual Gravity (N-body Physics)

The leap from "toy" to "real simulator." Currently only the black hole pulls
on things; every body lives in its own bubble, ignoring every other body.
By the end of Stage 2, every body pulls on every other body, mass actually
matters, and three bodies can do beautiful, chaotic things you didn't program.

This stage is conceptually meatier than Stage 1 even though the code growth
is moderate (~50–80 lines added). Most of the work is *restructuring* the
integrator to handle multiple bodies whose accelerations depend on each
other's positions.

---

## What changes, physically

In Stage 1, each body's acceleration depends only on its own position
relative to the (fixed) black hole. Bodies are independent. The integrator
loop is happily one-body-at-a-time:

```cpp
for (Body& b : bodies) integrate(b, blackHole, dt);
```

In Stage 2, each body feels the sum of pulls from the black hole **and from
every other body**. The acceleration on body *i* is

    a_i = a_from_BH(r_i)  +  Σ over j≠i of  G·m_j·(r_j − r_i) / |r_j − r_i|³

This means **you cannot integrate one body at a time anymore**. If you
update body 0's position first and then compute body 1's acceleration using
that already-moved body 0, you've broken the symmetry of the timestep and
your physics is wrong. Everyone has to step forward "simultaneously."

The fix is a two-pass pattern. First pass: compute accelerations for *all*
bodies using the *current* positions of all bodies. Second pass: apply
those accelerations and update positions. Verlet's kick-drift-kick becomes:

1. For all i: compute a_i at current positions
2. For all i: v_i += a_i · dt/2     ← half-kick
3. For all i: r_i += v_i · dt        ← drift
4. For all i: compute a_i at new positions
5. For all i: v_i += a_i · dt/2     ← half-kick

Notice we compute accelerations *twice* per step (steps 1 and 4). The
acceleration computation is now the most expensive thing your sim does.

---

## The cost of N-body

The pair loop computes interactions for every pair of bodies. With N bodies
you have N·(N−1)/2 pairs. That's **O(N²)**, which gets brutal fast:

|  N  |  Pair operations per frame  |
|-----|------------------------------|
|  10 |  45                          |
|  50 |  1,225                       |
| 100 |  4,950                       |
| 500 |  124,750                     |
|1000 |  499,500                     |

For your project's scope (a few dozen bodies), O(N²) is fine. Real research
N-body codes use Barnes-Hut trees (O(N log N)) or fast multipole methods,
but that's overkill here. **Knowing it's O(N²) and that you'd need
something smarter at 10,000 bodies is the lesson** — implementing it isn't.

The one optimization absolutely worth doing is **Newton's third law**. The
force body *j* exerts on *i* is equal and opposite to the force *i* exerts
on *j*. So when you compute the i-j interaction, you can write the result
into *both* accel[i] and accel[j] with opposite signs, halving the work.

```cpp
// for each pair i < j (compute once, apply to both)
Vec2 d   = r_j - r_i;
float r  = sqrt(d.x*d.x + d.y*d.y + EPS*EPS);
float invR3 = 1.0f / (r*r*r);
Vec2 force_dir = d * (G * invR3);    // direction & magnitude factor
accel[i] += force_dir * m_j;          // i pulled toward j
accel[j] -= force_dir * m_i;          // j pulled toward i (opposite)
```

---

## A subtle thing about mass

In Stage 1, your `b.mass = 1.0f` was unused — only the black hole's mass
affected anything. In Stage 2, **a body's mass is what makes it pull on
other bodies**. With your real SI units, a 1 kg body produces effectively
zero gravity. For mutual gravity to be observable, your bodies need realistic
astronomical masses. Suggested starting points:

| Body type     | Mass (kg)         |
|---------------|-------------------|
| "Spaceship"   |  10⁶  (a million kg, like a heavy spacecraft) |
| Asteroid      |  10¹⁷             |
| Moon          |  7.3 × 10²²       |
| Earth         |  5.97 × 10²⁴      |
| Sun (don't)   |  1.99 × 10³⁰      |

A spaceship barely perturbs anything. An Earth meaningfully tugs a passing
moon. Two Earths near each other do interesting choreography. Tuning these
is part of the stage — there's no "right" answer.

## Sub-steps

### Step 1: Refactor `integrate` into a two-pass `step` function

This is the biggest structural change in Stage 2. Move from "function takes
one body" to "function takes the whole vector of bodies." Suggested signature:

```cpp
void step(std::vector<Body>& bodies, const BlackHole& bh, float dt);
```

Inside, you do the five-step Verlet sequence above. You'll need somewhere to
store each body's acceleration during the step. Two options:

- Add `Vec2 acceleration;` as a field on `Body`
- Use a parallel local array `std::vector<Vec2> accel(bodies.size());`

The second is cleaner because acceleration isn't really part of a body's
state — it's a temporary computed each frame. I'd go with the local array.

**Commit:** "Stage 2: refactor integrator to vector-wide step function"

Test: the simulation should still behave *exactly* as it did at the end of
Stage 1, because you haven't added mutual gravity yet — just restructured.
This is a non-trivial test. If anything looks different, you broke
something in the refactor.

### Step 2: Add proper softening, retire the clamp

Replace your `if (r1 < 0.1f) r1 = 0.1f;` with the `+ EPS*EPS` term inside
the sqrt, in both the BH-gravity computation and the new mutual-gravity
computation you're about to write. Tune `EPS` (start at ~1e3 metres in
your SI units — about a kilometre — and adjust).

**Commit:** "Stage 2: proper softening replaces distance clamp"

### Step 3: Add naive mutual gravity (no third-law optimization yet)

Inside your `step` function, between "compute BH accels" and "apply
half-kick," add a nested loop:

```cpp
for (size_t i = 0; i < bodies.size(); i++) {
    for (size_t j = 0; j < bodies.size(); j++) {
        if (i == j) continue;
        // compute accel on i from j and add to accel[i]
    }
}
```

This is O(N²) and does each pair *twice*. Inefficient but easy to verify.
Run with two bodies and watch them orbit each other.

**Commit:** "Stage 2: mutual gravity (naive N² loop)"

### Step 4: Apply Newton's third law

Refactor the nested loop to iterate over each pair only once:

```cpp
for (size_t i = 0; i < bodies.size(); i++) {
    for (size_t j = i + 1; j < bodies.size(); j++) {
        // compute force-direction once, apply to both with opposite signs
    }
}
```

The simulation should behave identically to step 3, but with roughly half
the math per frame. Add a FPS counter (raylib's `DrawFPS`) to confirm.

**Commit:** "Stage 2: Newton's third law optimization"

### Step 5: Visual mass differentiation

A 10⁶ kg spaceship and a 5.97×10²⁴ kg Earth should not look the same. Map
mass to draw radius (logarithmically — linear would make Earth a million
times bigger than a spaceship). And map mass to color (e.g., smaller =
blue, larger = orange/red).

```cpp
float displayRadius = bodyWorldRadius * (1.0f + 0.3f * log10f(b.mass / 1e6f));
```

**Commit:** "Stage 2: bodies sized and colored by mass"

### Step 6: Bind keys to different masses

You currently spawn with key `1`. Make it: `1` spaceship (10⁶ kg),
`2` moon (7×10²² kg), `3` Earth (6×10²⁴ kg). This is the spec from the
original 3D plan finally arriving.

**Commit:** "Stage 2: keys spawn different-mass bodies"

### Step 7: The three-body experiment

Clear the scene. Drop two Earths and a moon, or arrange a manual setup.
Watch what happens. You'll observe **the three-body problem**:
unpredictable, chaotic, beautiful, sometimes spectacular ejections.

Optional: try to set up the famous **figure-8 three-body orbit** — three
equal masses chase each other along a figure-8 path forever. Initial
conditions (in scaled units, you'll need to convert):
- Body 1: position (−0.97000436, 0.24308753), velocity (0.466203685, 0.43236573)
- Body 2: position (0.97000436, −0.24308753), velocity (0.466203685, 0.43236573)
- Body 3: position (0, 0), velocity (−0.93240737, −0.86473146)

Whether it works depends on your units conversion, and getting it stable is
hard. It's a delightful side quest, not a requirement.

**Commit:** "Stage 2: three-body chaos experimentally observed"

## What C++ this stage exercises

- **Two-pass algorithms.** The Verlet refactor enforces it: compute all,
  then apply all. This pattern appears constantly in real systems work
  (graphics, physics, networking).
- **`std::vector` of derived data.** The local `accel` vector lives only
  inside `step()` — practice with stack-allocated containers and RAII.
- **`size_t` and signed/unsigned care.** Pair loops use `size_t` indices;
  watch for `i - 1` underflow if you ever subtract from a `size_t` at 0.
- **`const` correctness pays off.** Mark `step()`'s parameters thoughtfully:
  `bodies` mutates, `bh` doesn't. The compiler will catch your mistakes.
- **Performance awareness without premature optimization.** Implement
  naive, measure, then optimize the third-law speedup. Verify it actually
  helps via FPS.

## Gotchas you'll hit

**Bodies explode when they pass close to each other.** Increase `EPS`.

**Energy isn't conserved over long runs.** Two causes: (1) `dt` too large,
(2) softening too small. Verlet conserves energy beautifully only when
both are well-tuned for your scales.

**Three bodies seem to fly apart immediately.** Initial conditions matter
hugely. Random placement plus random velocities is a great way to produce
ejections. Stability needs care.

**Visual size of the moon makes it impossible to see when zoomed out.**
That's mass-vs-display tension. The log scaling formula keeps masses
visible across orders of magnitude.

**FPS tanks at 50+ bodies.** Expected. Profile if you want to see *why*
(it'll be the pair loop). The cure isn't writing assembly — it's spatial
data structures (Barnes-Hut), which is a Stage 2.5 side quest if you ever
want to do it.

## You're done with Stage 2 when

- Two Earths in the scene perturb each other visibly as they orbit the BH
- Three bodies do something interesting — orbit, chase, eject, capture
- The figure-8 orbit either works or you've made an honest attempt
- Bodies look different sizes and colors based on mass
- Your `step()` function reads cleanly and you can explain every line
- The original Stage 1 behavior still works (one body around BH, click-drag)
- 7+ commits, each a working state

## Time estimate

Given your Stage 1 pace: **2–4 days of focused work**. The refactor in
Step 1 is the time sink — restructuring code you understand is harder than
writing new code, because you have to hold both the old and the new
versions in your head simultaneously. Don't rush it. After Step 1 lands,
the rest cascades quickly.

## What comes after

Stage 3 is **promotion to 3D**. The physics barely changes — `Vec2` becomes
`Vec3`, all your sqrt-of-x²+y² becomes sqrt-of-x²+y²+z², all your
DrawCircle becomes DrawSphere. The rendering is the actual lift: a 3D
camera you can orbit with the mouse, perspective projection, depth. You'll
have a working tiny solar system around your black hole.

But that's two weeks from now. Stage 2 first.
